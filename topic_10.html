FCS Teaching Topics: Giving Better Feedback Directly on Students' Project Work to Reduce Your Workload

Recommendations.

Not everything on an assignment must have written feedback. Choose the most important points that will make a significant improvement in overall attainment and provide feedback on that.

Be specific with feedback, it must be accurate and valid to the task.

Formative feedback must shape learning and summative feedback must explain the outcome.

Formative assessment is ongoing, it takes place during the learning process whereas summative assessment takes place at the end of a unit or course (ie., projects, tests, exams). Typically, marks are given for summative assessments only. However, it is important to ensure that feedback given for both types of assessment is appropriate and meaningful to the learner. The feedback given on formative assessments should help the learner to improve their work/knowledge of course material in time to achieve better attainment in summative assessments. The goal is to improve students' learning which, ideally, should result in better attainment for the student.
Suggested below are some principles of feedback for consideration that are designed to enhance student learning and attainment
Principles of Feedback Designed to Enhance Assessment Practices (Nicol, 2008) - edited:
  • Clarify what good performance is (goals, criteria, standards).
  • Encourage time and effort on challenging learning tasks.
  • Be specific in feedback as to what needs to be improved for a better result
  • Provide opportunities to act on feedback (to improve between current and desired performance).
  • Encourage interaction and dialogue around learning (peer and teacher-student).
  • Encourage the positive motivational beliefs and self-esteem

What can you do to implement best practice of feedback in your class?

  • Ensure students know what they are supposed to do i.e., what it is that they are being assessed on. Rubrics must be clear! Students can better understand the assessment criteria by implementing a learning task using it. For example, show learners an example project (exemplar) and assess it together in class using the rubric. Engaging with the rubric will develop an understanding of the criteria and the various levels of possible achievement per criterion.
  • Engage students more fully in the assessment process. Carless (2007) cites a range of activities, such as the use of exemplars, peer feedback, peer assessment and self-evaluation skills designed to help students use feedback to ‘feedforward’ into summative work.
  • Incorporate the above (#2) type of activity within a ‘Community of Practice’ in order to develop a mutual understanding of tacit and explicit knowledge between the student and tutor in the assessment process O’Donovan et al. (2008) Incorporate #2 with the submitted assessment. It is crucial for the tutor/instructor to see that the student has gone through the process towards improvement in their learning.
  • Curtail summative feedback to ‘a few concise points for further development'. Whilst reducing summative feedback to minor points and/or a grade is controversial, it highlights the need to gain an effective balance between different forms of feedback.
