FCS Teaching Topics: Rubrics, Time Saving Techniques

TAKE-AWAYS

There are three different types of rubric (scroll down to see examples)
  1. Analytic Rubric - the most common type in higher education, very prescribed but can be extremely effective (Mertler, 2001).
  2. Holistic Rubric - describes criteria per a grade band, very broad based (University of Northern Florida, n.d.).
  3. Single Point - success criteria is set within a grade band. Opportunities for written feedback to students can be given on either side of the criteria for how they need to improve to meet the criteria or how they exceeded it (Fluckiger, 2010).
Factors that hinder rubrics
  1. Poorly designed rubrics (too much information per criterion or not enough)
  2. Lack of clarity in the criteria on what students need to do to succeed
  3. Omission of feedback to student on what it is they need to focus on to improve results

Analytic Rubrics

Source: http://www.queensu.ca/teachingandlearning/modules/assessments/35_s4_05_types_of_rubrics.html
Advantages
  1. An analytic rubric unpacks an assignment into parts (categories), allowing the instructor to itemize and define exactly what aspects are strong, and which ones need improvement by specifying criteria per category.
  2. It gives students a clearer picture of why they got the score they got - without having to explain everything in a later conversation.
  3. They are useful as they cover all possible outcomes. Time must be given to create clear performance levels to be clear on exactly what every level of performance looks like.
Disadvantages
  1. An Creating them takes time. Defining all the ways the work could go wrong, and all the ways it could exceed expectations, is a time consuming task
  2. Students won’t necessarily read (engage with) the rubric, meaning they won’t clearly understand what’s expected of them.
  3. Need for additional feedback. Many assessors still feel that they still provide some written feedback as they want to explain further why a student may have received a certain mark; the criteria doesn't always fit what a student has done in a specific category.

Holistic Rubrics

Source: https://www.slideshare.net/missreynova/a-sample-of-holistic-scoring-rubric
A holistic rubric is the most general kind. It lists three to five levels of performance, along with a broad description of the characteristics that define each level. The levels can be labeled with numbers (such as 1 through 6), letters (such as A through F) or words (such as Beginning through Exemplary). What each level is called isn’t what makes the rubric holistic — it’s the way the characteristics are all lumped together (Gonzales, 2014).
Advantages:
  1. It takes less time to create
  2. Typically less time to assess.
Disadvantages:
  1. It doesn't provide targeted feedback to students, they are unlikely to learn much about what they need to do to improve.
They are most useful in standardized assessments where feedback is not required.

Single Point Rubric - the best for last!

A single-point rubric is a simple and streamlined way to calibrate marking (very similar to an analytic rubric; as it unpacks the components of an assignment into various criteria. The clear difference is that it only describes the criteria for proficiency; it does not attempt to list all the ways a student could fall short, nor does it specify how a student could exceed expectations (University of North Florida, n.d.).
